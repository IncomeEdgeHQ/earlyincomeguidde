# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Picking Affiliate Products

You signed up for three affiliate programs, slapped some links in a blog post, and waited. Nothing happened. So you added more links. Still nothing. That's not a traffic problem — that's a product selection problem, and it's the reason most beginners waste their first three months on commissions they'll never see.

I did exactly this. Picked products based on what seemed popular, what had a decent-looking commission rate, and what I'd seen other people promote. I had no real system. And the results showed it.

This article is about fixing that before you waste your own three months.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners 3 Months

Most people start affiliate marketing the wrong way around. They find a product first, then try to build an audience around it. That's like buying a specific type of fishing rod before you know what lake you're fishing in.

The real starting point isn't the product. It's the problem.

What specific problem does your audience have? What are they Googling at 11pm when they're frustrated? What question do they keep asking in forums, Reddit threads, comment sections? Start there. When you understand the problem deeply, product selection becomes almost obvious — because you're looking for solutions that already exist, not casting around hoping something sticks.

This sounds basic. But almost every beginner skips it. And it's the single biggest reason their first campaigns go nowhere.

---

## Why Commission Rate Is the Wrong First Filter

Here's the thing nobody says out loud: a 50% commission on a product nobody buys is worth exactly zero.

High commission rates are seductive. They're the first thing you see in most affiliate dashboards, and they make you feel like you're being strategic when you sort by highest payout. But commission rate is a *lagging* indicator. It tells you what you *could* earn. It tells you nothing about whether anyone actually wants the product.

The filter you want to use first is buyer intent. Is there a clear, existing demand for this? Are people already searching for solutions in this category? Are there forums, YouTube channels, and subreddits full of people talking about this exact problem? High buyer intent with a decent commission will beat low buyer intent with a premium commission every single time.

Start with demand. Layer on commission rate after.

## The One Question That Separates Good Products From Bad Ones

Before you promote anything, ask yourself this: "Would I recommend this to a friend if there was no money involved?"

It sounds like a cliché, but it's the most useful filter I've ever used. Because if the honest answer is no — if you're only promoting it because the payout looks good — your content is going to reflect that. It'll feel hollow. Readers can sense when something is being sold at them versus genuinely recommended to them.

This matters more now than it did five years ago. People are more skeptical. They've been burned by "honest reviews" that clearly weren't honest. When you promote something you actually believe in, you write about it differently. You mention specific details. You describe the use case accurately. You flag the downsides. That kind of content builds trust, and trust is what converts.

There's also a practical angle here. Products you understand well are products you can write about consistently and accurately. That means better content, better SEO, better conversions. It all connects.

## Physical vs Digital vs Recurring: Know What You're Getting Into

Not all affiliate products are built the same, and the type of product you choose shapes your entire strategy.

**Physical products** (Amazon Associates being the classic example) tend to have low commission rates — often 1-5%. The trust barrier is low because people already buy physical goods online constantly. But you're working hard for small payouts, and you need volume.

**Digital products** — courses, ebooks, software — typically pay 20-50% and sometimes more. The trust barrier is higher because people are more cautious about digital purchases. But one sale can earn you what fifty Amazon clicks might earn.

**Recurring commissions** are where things get interesting for the long game. SaaS tools, membership platforms, subscription services — these pay you every month the customer stays subscribed. Promote something once, get paid monthly. Your income compounds instead of resetting to zero every month. If you're thinking about affiliate marketing as an actual income stream rather than a side experiment, recurring products should be on your radar from day one.

The right mix depends on your niche and how you're building your audience. But going in with eyes open about the economics of each type saves you from a lot of frustration later.

## How To Stress-Test a Product Before You Commit

So you've found a product that solves a real problem, has decent buyer intent, pays reasonably well, and you actually believe in it. Before you go all in, do a quick stress test.

First, check the sales page. Is it convincing? Does it answer objections? Does it make a clear case for the product? Because when you send traffic to a bad sales page, you're working against yourself. You can write the best pre-sell content in the world, and a weak landing page will kill your conversion rate.

Second, look for social proof. Reviews, testimonials, active community — these tell you the product has real users and delivers on its promises. A product with no reviews is a risk.

Third, look at the affiliate terms carefully. Some programs have cookie durations as short as 24 hours (yes, Amazon, I'm looking at you). Others offer 30, 60, even 90-day cookies. Longer cookies mean more time for a referred visitor to convert, which directly affects your earnings. Check refund rates if you can find them. And check whether the company actually supports its affiliates with creatives, data, and communication — or whether you're completely on your own once you sign up.

## The Niche Trap That Keeps You Stuck in Research Mode

One more thing worth naming: paralysis by niche.

You've probably seen the advice to "pick a niche" before you do anything else. And yes, focus matters. But a lot of beginners spend weeks — sometimes months — trying to find the *perfect* niche before they start. They research, make lists, second-guess themselves, and never actually publish anything.

Here's what I'd tell myself if I could go back: start with what you genuinely know, care about, or are actively learning. Not what you think will make the most money. You'll iterate and refine your niche naturally as you produce content and see what resonates. The people who make real progress in affiliate marketing are the ones who started *imperfectly* and adjusted, not the ones who planned perfectly and never published.

Pick a direction. Choose one product that genuinely fits. Write about it honestly. That's the actual starting point.
