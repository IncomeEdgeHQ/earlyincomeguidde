# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Picking Affiliate Products

You signed up for three affiliate programs, slapped some links in a blog post, and waited. Nothing happened. So you signed up for three more programs, wrote more posts, added more links — and still nothing.

That was me for the first four months. And the frustrating part is that nobody told me the real reason it wasn't working had nothing to do with my writing, my traffic, or my SEO.

It was the products I was choosing.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners 3 Months

Most beginners — myself included — pick affiliate products based on commission rate. You see 40% recurring commission and your brain does the math. *If I get 100 sales a month...* You know the fantasy.

Here's the problem: high commission rates mean nothing if the product doesn't convert, doesn't match your audience, or doesn't solve a real problem your readers actually have right now.

I promoted a $97 software tool for months because the affiliate payout was great. I got exactly two clicks and zero sales. Meanwhile, a simple $29 ebook I mentioned once in passing — because I genuinely used it — made me six sales in two weeks.

The difference wasn't the price. It wasn't the commission. It was fit.

Choosing the right product before you create a single piece of content will save you months of wasted effort. And that's what the rest of this is about.

---

## Why "Just Promote What You Like" Is Half the Answer

You've probably heard the advice: *promote products you actually use and believe in.* And honestly? That advice is solid — but it's incomplete.

Authenticity matters. If you're faking enthusiasm for something you've never touched, readers feel it. The writing goes flat. The recommendation lacks specificity. You can't answer a question in the comments because you don't actually know the product.

But here's what that advice leaves out: you also need to promote things that align with what your audience is *actively searching for and trying to solve*. Loving a product isn't enough if nobody in your corner of the internet is looking for it.

The sweet spot is a product you've used or genuinely trust *and* that solves a specific, urgent pain point your reader is already experiencing. Both things have to be true. One without the other is a coin flip.

---

## The 3-Question Filter That Changed How I Pick Products

Before I commit to promoting anything now, I run it through three questions. This takes five minutes and has saved me from a lot of dead-end content.

**1. Does this solve a specific, named problem?**
Not "helps with productivity" — that's too vague. Something like "helps freelancers track billable hours without a spreadsheet" is specific. The more specific the problem, the easier it is to find people who have it and write content that actually reaches them.

**2. Would I recommend this to a friend without being paid to?**
This one sounds soft but it's genuinely useful. If the answer is "I mean, it's fine I guess," that hesitation will leak into your content. Readers pick up on lukewarm energy.

**3. Is there search demand or audience interest I can actually reach?**
Look at Google, Reddit, YouTube, or whatever platform you're building on. Are people asking questions this product answers? You don't need huge traffic — you need *relevant* traffic. A niche product with a passionate audience beats a mass-market product every time.

If a product clears all three, it goes on my list. If it fails any of them, I leave it for now — even if the commission is tempting.

## The Niche Trap Nobody Warns You About

Here's one nobody says out loud: being too broad kills your affiliate results just as fast as choosing bad products.

When I started, my blog covered personal finance, productivity, side hustles, tech tools, and self-improvement. Every post felt slightly disconnected from the last. Readers didn't know what I was about. Worse — *I* didn't know what I was about. So the products I promoted were all over the place too.

The fix isn't to niche down so hard that you're writing for an audience of twelve people. It's about having a clear content lane. Mine eventually became: earning more money and managing it better as a regular person without a finance degree. That's a lane. Everything I promote now lives inside that lane.

When your content has a lane, the right affiliate products become much more obvious. You're not asking "what could I promote?" You're asking "what do people in my lane need?" That's a much easier question to answer.

## How To Evaluate a Product Before You Promote It

Let's get practical. You've found a product that feels right. Here's how to actually vet it before you put your name behind it.

**Check the sales page yourself.** Would you buy this? Is the copy clear, the pricing fair, the guarantee reasonable? If the sales page looks sketchy to you, imagine how a first-time visitor feels.

**Look for real reviews outside the affiliate ecosystem.** Reddit is gold for this. Search the product name + "review" or "honest" and see what real users say. Not the polished testimonials on the product page — the random comment at 11pm from someone who's frustrated or thrilled.

**Test the product if you can.** Many SaaS tools offer free trials. Ebooks are cheap enough to just buy. If you can't afford to test it, at minimum find two or three people who have used it and ask them directly. This kind of due diligence makes your content 10x more credible.

**Look at the affiliate terms.** Cookie duration matters more than most beginners realize. A 24-hour cookie (looking at you, Amazon) means someone can click your link, wait two days, buy the product, and you get nothing. A 30, 60, or 90-day cookie gives you a much better shot at actually getting credited for the sale.

## The Content-Product Match Problem

Even after you've picked the right product, there's one more piece most beginners skip — and it's the reason good products still don't convert.

Your content has to match the buying intent of your reader *at the moment they land on your page.*

Someone reading a "beginner's guide to affiliate marketing" is in research mode. They're not ready to buy a $200 tool yet. But someone reading "best email marketing platforms for affiliate marketers" is comparison-shopping. They're close to a decision. A direct recommendation with a link makes complete sense there.

This is called matching the product to the funnel stage. And it's the reason I stopped throwing affiliate links into every post and started being much more intentional about which posts I optimize for conversion versus which ones I optimize for trust-building.

## What To Do Next

Here's where to start — this week, not someday.

**1. Audit the affiliate products you're already promoting (or planning to).** Run each one through the three-question filter. Be honest. If a product fails the filter, put it on pause and stop creating content around it.

**2. Get specific about your content lane.** Write one sentence that describes who your content is for and what problem it helps them solve. If you can't write that sentence clearly, your product choices will stay scattered.

**3. Find one product that clears the filter and actually test it yourself.** Then write one piece of content — a tutorial, a comparison, a case study — that matches high buying intent. Don't start with ten products. Start with one and do it properly.

Affiliate marketing works. But it works because of decisions made before the content is written, not because of tricks buried inside it. Get the product choice right first, and the rest gets a whole lot easier.
