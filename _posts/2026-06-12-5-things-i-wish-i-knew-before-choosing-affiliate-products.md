# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Choosing Affiliate Products

You picked a product, built a whole content plan around it, and then made absolutely nothing. Not because you were lazy. Not because your writing was bad. Because you chose the wrong product from day one — and nobody told you that was even a thing to worry about.

That's the quiet killer in affiliate marketing. Everyone teaches you *how* to promote. Almost nobody teaches you what to promote *first* — and why that single decision determines whether you're earning in 60 days or still grinding six months later wondering what you're missing.

I've been there. Here's what I wish someone had just told me straight.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners 3 Months

The first affiliate product I promoted was a premium SaaS tool I'd barely used. It had a great commission rate — 40% recurring — and that number looked incredible on paper. So I built content around it, wrote comparison posts, even made a tutorial.

Zero sales.

Here's what I didn't understand yet: commission rate means nothing if there's no trust behind your recommendation. I wasn't using the tool daily. I couldn't answer basic questions about it in the comments. And readers — even casual ones — can *feel* that.

The beginner trap is optimizing for commission percentage before everything else. It feels logical. Higher payout, less work needed, right? Wrong. A product you genuinely use and understand will always convert better than a high-ticket item you're just pointing at. Start there. That's the foundation everything else is built on.

---

## Why "Pick Your Niche First" Is Half the Story

You've heard the advice: pick a niche, stay consistent, build authority. And yeah, that's directionally correct. But there's a missing step that most guides skip over entirely.

You don't just need a niche. You need a niche *with buyers in it*.

Not every niche has an audience that spends money online. Some niches have passionate communities full of people who will never click a buy button. Others have smaller but deeply motivated audiences who are actively looking for solutions — and will pay for them.

Before you commit to a direction, ask yourself: what does this audience *already spend money on*? If the answer is "not much" or "they prefer free resources," that's a signal worth listening to. A great test is to go into forums, subreddits, or Facebook groups in your target niche and just look at what people are *asking for help with*. If problems keep coming up and people are actively seeking paid tools, courses, or services to fix those problems — that's a buying audience.

That distinction alone will save you a lot of wasted effort.

---

## The Product Fit Test Nobody Talks About

Once you've got a niche you believe in, there's a simple question I now run every potential affiliate product through before I commit a single piece of content to it:

*Would I recommend this to a friend — and be comfortable with them coming back to me if it didn't work out?*

That sounds soft, but it's actually a pretty rigorous filter. It means you have to know the product well enough to stand behind it. It means the product needs to deliver on what it promises. And it means you're thinking about your long-term reputation, not just a quick commission.

Here's the practical version of that test:

- **Does the product solve a specific, real problem?** Vague value props don't convert.
- **Is there a clear "before and after" you can describe?** If you can't articulate the transformation, neither can your reader.
- **Does it have decent reviews outside of affiliate circles?** Check Trustpilot, Reddit, and YouTube comments — not just the vendor's testimonials page.
- **Is the price point reasonable for the audience?** A $497 product aimed at total beginners is a hard sell, no matter how good the commission is.

Run every product through that. You'll cut your shortlist in half — and the half you keep will actually perform.

## Low Ticket vs. High Ticket: What Actually Makes Sense When You're Starting Out

This debate gets heated in affiliate marketing communities, and honestly, both camps have a point. But here's my honest take for where you probably are right now.

High-ticket affiliate products (think $500+) sound appealing because one sale can mean $100–$200 in your pocket. But they typically require a warmer, more trusting audience to convert. If you're still building that audience — which you are, because you're a beginner — the conversion cycle is long and the feedback loop is slow. You might go weeks without data on whether your content is even working.

Low-to-mid ticket products ($20–$150) give you faster feedback. You can see what's resonating, what's converting, what's not — and adjust without spending months waiting on a handful of high-ticket conversions.

My suggestion? Start with products in the $30–$100 range with decent commission rates (20–40%). Build your first wins. Learn what your audience responds to. Then layer in higher-ticket products once you understand your traffic and your readers' buying behaviour.

## Recurring Commissions Are Worth More Than You Think

Let me say something that took me embarrassingly long to figure out.

A product that pays you $15/month every month is worth more than a product that pays you a $50 one-time commission — assuming even modest retention.

Recurring affiliate programs (usually software, membership sites, and tools) compound over time. You make a sale in month one, and that sale keeps paying you in month six, month twelve, sometimes longer. Your income builds a base instead of resetting to zero every month.

This doesn't mean ignore one-time products. But when you're evaluating your mix, deliberately include at least one or two recurring-commission programs. That's the unsexy, underrated move that separates people who've been doing this for a year and made $8,000 from people who've been doing it a year and made $800.

Look for SaaS tools, email platforms, membership communities, and hosting services in your niche. Most have affiliate programs. Many pay monthly recurring commissions and will tell you so directly on their affiliate program page.

## The Volume Trap (And Why One Great Product Beats Ten Mediocre Ones)

When you're starting out, there's a temptation to sign up for every affiliate program you can find. Spread your bets. More products, more chances, right?

In practice it backfires almost every time.

Promoting ten products means you're spreading your content, your focus, and your credibility thin across all ten. You become a catalogue instead of a trusted voice. Your reader can't figure out what you actually stand behind — so they click nothing.

Pick two or three products maximum when you're getting started. Go deep on them. Write genuinely helpful content around the problems they solve. Build FAQs, comparisons, and tutorials. Become the person who clearly *knows* this product inside and out.

That depth of coverage is what builds search traffic over time and, more importantly, what builds reader trust. One affiliate relationship done well will beat ten half-hearted ones every single time.

## Your Next Step

You don't need a perfect strategy. You need to start with the right one product, and build from there.

Here's what to do right now:

1. **List 3–5 products you already use and genuinely like** — tools, apps, courses, services. Check if they have affiliate programs. This is your starting pool. Real experience beats research every time.

2. **Run each one through the product fit test** from earlier in this article. Cross off anything you can't confidently recommend with your name attached to it.

3. **Pick one. Just one.** Write your first piece of content around a real problem it solves. Don't build a website first, don't design a logo, don't set up all your social accounts. Write the content. Ship it. Learn from what happens.

That's it. That's the whole unlock.

Affiliate marketing isn't magic, but it's also not as complicated as the overcrowded content space makes it look. You just have to start with products worth promoting — and you're already thinking about that, which puts you ahead of most people who jumped straight to tactics.

You've got this.
