# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Picking Affiliate Products

You signed up for three affiliate programs in one afternoon, slapped some links into a blog post, and waited. Nothing happened. So you signed up for three more.

That was me for the first four months. And the painful truth is that the product selection part — the very first decision you make — is where most beginners quietly wreck their chances before they've written a single word of content.

Nobody talks about this enough. The tutorials jump straight to traffic and SEO, but if you're promoting the wrong products from the start, more traffic just means more people ignoring your links faster.

Let's fix that.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners 3 Months

Here's what almost everyone does: they Google "best affiliate programs for beginners," find a list, and pick whatever has the highest commission percentage.

That's backwards.

High commission means nothing if you can't write about the product authentically, if nobody's searching for it, or if it solves a problem your audience doesn't actually have. I once spent six weeks building content around a project management SaaS tool because it paid 40% recurring commissions. I had zero experience with it. The content was hollow. The conversions were zero.

The real question isn't *what pays the most* — it's *what can I speak to with enough confidence that someone trusts my recommendation*. That shift in thinking changes everything about how you approach product selection.

---

## Why "Picking Your Niche" Advice Is Incomplete

You've heard it a hundred times: pick a niche. Personal finance. Health. Tech. Got it.

But niche alone doesn't tell you which *products within that niche* are worth your time. And that middle layer — product-level research — is where most beginner guides just... stop.

Here's what actually matters at the product level:

**Does the product have a proven sales page?** If the vendor's own landing page is confusing, outdated, or unclear about what you get, their conversion rate is going to tank your efforts even if your traffic is solid. You're not just selling — you're sending people to someone else's sales process.

**Is there search intent behind the problems it solves?** Go into Google and search the problem the product fixes, not the product name itself. If people are searching for the problem in volume, you have something to work with. If they're not, you're fighting uphill.

**Can you actually test it or speak to it honestly?** This is the one nobody wants to admit. We've all been tempted to promote something based on the affiliate page alone. But readers can smell that. Even a free trial, a demo, or reading genuine user reviews deeply enough to synthesize a real opinion — that matters.

---

## The Commission Structure Nobody Explains Properly

Let me break this down simply because it confused me for way too long.

There are basically three types of commission structures you'll encounter: one-time flat commissions, recurring commissions, and tiered commissions. Each one changes your strategy.

**One-time commissions** (like Amazon Associates or most physical product programs) pay you once per sale. These can add up, but they require consistent volume. If you're just starting out and don't have steady traffic yet, one sale here and there won't feel like much.

**Recurring commissions** are the ones worth getting excited about. These come from SaaS tools, membership programs, or subscription services where you earn a percentage every month the customer stays subscribed. One conversion that sticks can pay you for a year. This is where I'd focus most of your early energy.

**Tiered commissions** scale your rate based on how many sales you generate. Great in theory, but not where you want to put your attention when you're starting from zero — you won't hit the good tiers for a while anyway.

For beginners, my honest recommendation: find two or three recurring commission products in your niche and build your early content around those. You want each piece of content you create to have compounding earning potential, not just a one-shot payout.

## How To Vet a Product Before You Commit Time To It

This is the actual process I use now — and it takes maybe 30 minutes per product.

**Step 1 — Search Reddit and forums first.** Go to Reddit and search the product name. Real users are brutally honest there. You'll learn the actual objections people have, the real benefits, and whether people actually like it or just tolerate it. This also gives you language you can use in your content.

**Step 2 — Check the affiliate program terms carefully.** Cookie duration matters more than most beginners realise. A 24-hour cookie (looking at you, Amazon) means if someone clicks your link and buys three days later, you get nothing. Look for 30-day minimum, ideally 60-90 days. Also check for payout thresholds — some programs won't pay you until you hit $100, which can take a while when you're new.

**Step 3 — Look at the product's review profile across the web.** Trustpilot, G2, Capterra — wherever reviews exist for that product. You're not looking for perfection. You're looking for a pattern. If the bad reviews are all about one specific thing, that's actually useful content fodder. You can write "Is [Product] worth it?" honestly.

**Step 4 — Check if the affiliate program is direct or through a network.** Networks like ShareASale, Impact, or PartnerStack give you tracking dashboards, reliable payments, and often more professional affiliate support. Direct programs can be just as good, but the onboarding and tracking can be inconsistent.

## The "Buyer Intent" Filter That Changes Your Content Strategy

Here's a concept that took me embarrassingly long to understand: not all content brings in buyers.

Informational content (like "what is affiliate marketing") attracts learners. Buyer intent content (like "best email marketing tools for bloggers" or "[Product] review 2025") attracts people who are already close to a decision.

When you're choosing products to promote, you need to check whether buyer intent search terms actually exist around that product. Use a free tool like Google's autocomplete, or Ubersuggest's free tier. Search "[product name] review," "[product name] vs [competitor]," "best [category] tools," "is [product name] worth it."

If those searches exist and have some volume, you have a content strategy. If they don't, you're going to have to work much harder to create that intent from scratch — and that's a very long game for a beginner.

## Quantity Is a Trap. Start With Two Products.

I know the instinct. You want to diversify, cover your bases, test everything at once.

But spreading yourself across eight affiliate products when you're new means you create thin, uncommitted content about all of them. You learn nothing about what actually converts. And you burn out in month two.

Pick two products. Maybe three, maximum. Write multiple pieces of content about each one — a review, a comparison, a "how I use it" post, a problem-focused article that leads back to it. Build genuine depth around a small number of things.

This is how you actually learn what works. When one product starts converting and another doesn't, you'll have real data to act on. That data is worth more than any course or guide at this stage.

The beginner trap is always more — more programs, more content topics, more niches. The real move is less, done better.

## Your Next Step

Here's what I'd actually do if I were starting from scratch today:

**1. List 5 products you already use or have genuine opinions about** — tools, subscriptions, resources, anything. Check if they have affiliate programs. Start there. Authenticity is your only edge when you have no audience and no authority yet.

**2. Run each product through the 4-step vetting process above.** Spend 30 minutes per product. You'll immediately eliminate two or three of them, and that's a win — you just saved yourself weeks of wasted content creation.

**3. Build a simple comparison tracker.** Cookie duration, commission type, payout threshold, buyer intent search terms.
