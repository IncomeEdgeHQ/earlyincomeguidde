# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Choosing Affiliate Products

You picked a product, slapped a link in your content, and waited. Nothing happened. So you picked another product, wrote more content, waited again — and still nothing.

That was me for the first four months. And the frustrating part is that I wasn't lazy about it. I was *busy* about it, which is a completely different thing.

The problem wasn't my traffic. It wasn't my writing. It was that I'd skipped the one step that actually determines whether any of this works: choosing the right product before you do anything else. Nobody really talks about this part in enough detail. They jump straight to SEO, email lists, content calendars — and you're left wondering why your perfectly written review post is earning you exactly zero dollars.

Let me walk you through what I've learned the hard way.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners Three Months

Most people start by asking "what has the highest commission?" That's the wrong question — and it's costing you time you can't get back.

High commission products are almost always high-friction products. They're expensive, they require trust, and they need a warm audience before someone's going to click *and* buy. If you're brand new with no audience, promoting a $500 course at 40% commission sounds amazing on paper. In practice, you'll make nothing and feel defeated.

The better question to start with is: *"What would someone in my position actually buy, right now, after reading a helpful post?"*

That's the sweet spot. Low to mid-ticket products ($20–$150), high buyer intent, and low enough risk that a stranger will trust your recommendation without needing to know you for six months first. You're building momentum here, not a retirement fund. Get a few wins under your belt, then scale up.

---

## Why "Promoting What You Love" Advice Is Half-Right

You've heard it a hundred times: only promote products you use yourself. That advice is well-meaning but incomplete — and following it blindly will steer you wrong.

Loving a product doesn't mean there's a market for it. Loving a product doesn't mean the affiliate program is worth joining. And loving a product doesn't mean the people reading your content are ready to buy it. I genuinely loved a notebook app I promoted for two months. The commission was $3. The payout threshold was $50. I never saw a cent.

The real version of this advice is: **promote products you understand *and* that your audience has a real reason to buy.** That means combining personal familiarity with actual market research — not just enthusiasm.

Ask yourself three things before you commit to promoting anything:
1. Does this solve a specific, painful problem?
2. Are people actively searching for solutions to that problem?
3. Does the product's price match the trust level you've currently built?

If you can't answer yes to all three, keep looking.

## The Niche Trap Nobody Warns You About

Here's something the "pick a niche" crowd skips over: some niches are absolutely flooded with affiliate marketers, and some have almost none. Neither extreme is where you want to be.

Over-saturated niches — think weight loss, generic personal finance, broad "make money online" — are brutal for beginners. You're competing against people with five-year-old domains, email lists in the tens of thousands, and full-time content teams. You will not out-SEO them in month one. You just won't.

Under-served niches sound appealing until you realise there's a reason nobody's promoting in them: there's no buying intent. People browse, they don't buy.

What you're looking for is a **niche with proven buyers but room for a fresh voice**. Specific personal finance topics work well here — things like budgeting for freelancers, first-time investing for people in their 30s, or side hustle tools for parents with limited time. These have real search volume, real buyer intent, and they're not dominated by mega-sites. ## How to Actually Vet an Affiliate Program (Before You Waste a Month)

Not all affiliate programs are created equal, and this is where a lot of beginners get burned quietly. You write the content, you get the clicks, and then... the program is badly run, the cookies last 24 hours, or the minimum payout means you're waiting until you've earned $200 before you see anything.

Here's my quick vetting checklist before I join any program now:

**Cookie duration** — Anything under 30 days is a yellow flag. 60–90 days is solid. Amazon's 24-hour cookie is why so many people eventually move on from it.

**Commission rate vs. product price** — A 5% commission on a $300 product ($15) is more valuable than a 30% commission on a $10 product ($3). Do the actual maths.

**Payout threshold and frequency** — Some programs only pay out monthly, with a $100 minimum. That's fine when you're earning, but painful when you're starting out. Know this before you commit.

**Affiliate support and resources** — Programs that give you banners, copy ideas, and email swipes are genuinely easier to work with. It's not glamorous, but it matters.

**Refund rates** — If you can find out (sometimes you have to ask or read forums), high refund rates mean chargebacks on your commissions. Happened to me twice before I learned to check.

## The "One Product, One Problem" Rule That Changed Everything

For my first eight months, I was promoting five or six products across a handful of topics. I had zero depth and zero authority. I looked like someone who'd just discovered affiliate marketing rather than someone worth listening to.

The shift came when I committed to one product category, went deep on one problem, and built multiple pieces of content that all connected. A beginner's guide, a comparison post, a "is it worth it" review, and a tools roundup — all pointing to one core recommendation.

This is the compound effect in content form. Each piece supports the others. Your internal linking gets stronger. Google starts to understand what you're actually about. And readers who find you through one post naturally flow toward your recommendation.

It also makes you *better* at promoting. When you know one product inside out, you write better reviews, you answer the real objections, and you build the kind of credibility that converts.

Start with one product. Get one win. Then expand.

---

## What Beginners Get Wrong About "Passive Income"

I'll be straight with you: affiliate marketing is not passive in the beginning. Not even close. The passive part comes *after* you've put in the active work of building content, testing what converts, and finding your audience.

The reason this matters for product selection is that it should change your urgency. If you're treating this as a get-rich-quick thing, you'll keep chasing high-commission products that are hard to sell and give up before anything lands. If you treat it as building something real, you'll make smarter product choices, play the longer game, and actually stick around long enough to win.

The beginners who burn out fastest are the ones who chose products for the potential commission cheque. The ones who build something that lasts chose products based on genuine audience fit — and then stayed patient.

---

## Your Next Step

If you've been stuck in the "pick a product and hope" loop, here's what to do right now:

**1. Audit what you're currently promoting.** Run your existing (or planned) products against the three questions from earlier — painful problem, active searchers, right price for your trust level. Be honest. Cut what doesn't fit.

**2. Pick one product and map three content pieces around it.** A beginner's guide, a review, and a comparison post. That's your starting framework. Build depth before you build breadth.

**3. Check your affiliate program's cookie duration, payout threshold, and commission rate.** If you haven't done this, do it today. You might be promoting something that was never going to pay you properly regardless of how hard you worked.

You don't need to figure all of this out before you start. But you do need to get the product selection right before anything else makes sense. Get that foundation solid — everything else gets easier from there.
