# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Choosing Affiliate Products

You picked a product, built a whole content plan around it, and then made exactly zero sales. Not because your content was bad. Because you chose the wrong product before you even started.

That's the part nobody warns you about. Everyone teaches you *how* to promote affiliate products. Almost nobody teaches you how to pick the right ones in the first place — and that single gap is responsible for most beginners burning out in the first three months.

I learned this the expensive way. I spent weeks writing comparison articles for a software tool that paid a flat $5 commission. Flat. Not recurring. Five dollars. I had no idea there was a better way to think about this until I'd already wasted a chunk of time I'll never get back.

So let's fix that before you make the same call.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners 3 Months

Most beginners approach product selection backwards. They find a topic they like, Google "affiliate programs for [topic]," grab the first result, and start creating content. That's a strategy — just not a good one.

The problem is you're optimising for familiarity, not profit or fit. You're picking what's easy to find, not what's worth your time to promote.

Here's the mental shift that actually matters: your content is an asset. Every article, video, or post you create costs you time and sometimes money. Before you spend that, you need to know whether the product on the other end can actually pay you back. That means thinking about commission structure, buyer intent, and whether real people actually buy this thing — before you write a single word.

The good news is this is a quick filter once you know what to look for. Let's go through it.

---

## Why Commission Rate Isn't the Only Number That Matters

Most beginners fixate on commission percentage. 30% sounds great. 5% sounds terrible. But that framing is too simple, and it'll send you in the wrong direction.

What you actually want to look at is **commission value per conversion** — the real money that lands in your account when someone buys.

A 50% commission on a $10 product is $5. A 20% commission on a $200 product is $40. Same effort to promote. Very different outcome. And if that $200 product has a recurring subscription model? You could be earning $40 every month from a single customer you referred once.

That's the word that changes everything in affiliate marketing: **recurring**. If a program pays you ongoing commissions for as long as a customer stays subscribed, your income compounds over time instead of resetting to zero every month. When you're starting out, look specifically for SaaS tools, membership platforms, and subscription services in your niche. They're not always the easiest to promote, but they build real income momentum.

One more thing to check: cookie duration. This is how long after someone clicks your link you still get credit for the sale. A 24-hour cookie (like Amazon's) means if someone clicks and waits two days to buy, you get nothing. A 30 or 90-day cookie gives you a much more realistic shot at earning the commission. It's a small detail that has a massive impact on your actual conversion rate.

## The Trust Problem Nobody Talks About

Here's something the "just start affiliate marketing" crowd glosses over: if you promote something you don't believe in, your audience will feel it.

Not immediately. Not in a dramatic way. But over time, your content starts to feel hollow. The enthusiasm isn't there. The specific details that make people trust a recommendation — the ones that only come from actually using a product — are missing.

I'm not saying you need to buy everything you promote. That's not realistic, especially starting out. But you should be able to answer this honestly: *Would I recommend this to a friend if there was no commission involved?*

If the answer is a hard no, move on. There are thousands of affiliate programs. You don't need to attach your name to something you'd be embarrassed to stand behind.

If the answer is yes — or even "probably yes" — that's a product worth exploring further. You'll write about it better, you'll sound more convincing, and you'll attract buyers who actually trust you. That trust compounds too.

## How To Actually Find Products Worth Promoting

Okay, so where do you look? Here's the honest answer: start with products and tools you already use or have researched heavily.

Open your email subscriptions, your bookmarks, your app store. What are you actually paying for? What's solved a real problem for you? Those are your first candidates. Check if they have affiliate programs — most SaaS products do, often hidden in the footer under "Affiliates" or "Partners."

Beyond that, there are a few places worth knowing:

- **ShareASale** — huge network, good for physical products and software both
- **Impact.com** — a lot of mid-to-large brands run programs here
- **PartnerStack** — this is the one for SaaS tools specifically, and it's worth bookmarking if you're in the tech or productivity space
- **CJ Affiliate** — older platform but still solid for established brands
- **Direct programs** — often the best commissions come from going straight to the brand's website

When you find a program, check the commission rate, cookie duration, average order value if listed, and whether it's recurring. Also Google "[product name] affiliate program review" — other affiliates often share honest breakdowns of what actually converts and what the payout process is like.

## Matching Products To Where You Actually Show Up

This is the piece most beginner guides skip entirely: not every product works on every platform.

If you're writing long-form blog content, you can promote almost anything because you have space to explain, compare, and build trust before the click. If you're on TikTok or Instagram with short-form content, you need products that are visually demonstrable, emotionally resonant, or solve an obvious problem that's easy to show in 60 seconds.

Think about your content format before you lock in a product category. A complex B2B software tool might be a great affiliate offer — but it's nearly impossible to sell through short videos to a cold audience. A $40 physical product that visibly solves an annoying everyday problem? That can explode on short-form social.

Ask yourself: Can I explain the value of this product in the format I'm creating? If the answer is no, it doesn't matter how good the commission is.

Also think about buyer intent. Blog readers searching "best email marketing software for beginners" are in research mode and close to a decision. Social media followers are browsing and not actively shopping. You'll need to work differently — more entertainment, more emotion, more demonstration — to convert cold audiences. Match your product to where your audience's head is at.

## The Simple Filter That Eliminates Bad Choices Fast

When you're staring at a shortlist of possible products and can't decide, run each one through these five questions:

1. Does this solve a specific, real problem?
2. Would I recommend it without a commission?
3. Does the commission value make sense for the effort to promote it?
4. Is the cookie duration at least 30 days?
5. Are other affiliates actually making money with this? (Check forums, Reddit, YouTube)

If a product gets a yes on four or five of these, it's worth testing. If it's hitting two or three, keep looking. There's no rule that says you have to promote the first thing you find.

And test is the key word. You won't know for certain what converts until you put content out there. But choosing products with these filters means your tests are worth running — you're not just rolling the dice.

## Your Next Step

You don't need to have this perfectly figured out before you start. But you do need to choose deliberately.

Here's what to actually do today:

**Step 1:** List three tools, products, or services you currently use and would recommend to someone you like. Check if each one has an affiliate program. Start there — not with what pays the most, but with what you already trust.

**Step 2:** For each one, check commission rate, cookie duration, and whether it's recurring or one-time. Rank them by commission value per conversion, not by percentage alone.

**Step 3:** Pick one. Just one. Decide what format you'll create content in, and write your first piece of content with genuine intent to help the reader — not to sell them.

That's it. You'll learn more from publishing one piece of focused content around a well-chosen product than from a month of research paralysis.

You've already made the most important move, which is learning this before you wasted months on the wrong products. Now go use it.
