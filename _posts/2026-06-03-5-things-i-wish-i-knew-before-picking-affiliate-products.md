# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Picking Affiliate Products

You picked a product, slapped a link in your bio, and waited. Nothing happened. So you picked another product. Still nothing. Three months later you've promoted eight different things and earned exactly zero dollars — and you're starting to wonder if affiliate marketing is even real.

Here's the thing nobody tells you upfront: the product selection phase is where most beginners either win or lose before they've written a single word of content.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners 3 Months

Most people start by finding a product they like and then trying to build an audience around it. That's completely backwards.

The real sequence is: find an audience with a specific problem, figure out what they're already buying to solve it, and *then* find the affiliate product that fits that gap. When you flip those two steps, everything gets harder for no reason.

I made this exact mistake. I signed up for an Amazon Associates account, grabbed links for stuff I personally used — a standing desk, some supplements, a book — and sprinkled them into content. The conversion rate was embarrassing. Not because the products were bad. Because I hadn't thought for one second about who I was trying to reach or whether those people were actively looking for those things.

The audience-first mindset changes everything. And once you accept it, the whole product selection process becomes a lot less random.

---

## Why "High Commission" Is a Trap for New Affiliates

When you first start researching affiliate programs, you'll get dazzled by commission rates. 40%. 50%. Some recurring SaaS products offering 30% monthly. It looks incredible on paper.

But commission rate is basically irrelevant if the product doesn't convert.

A 50% commission on a product nobody wants to buy is still zero dollars. Meanwhile, a 10% commission on a product that solves a painful, urgent problem and has a well-built sales page? That's where you actually make money.

There are two things that matter way more than the commission percentage:

**Conversion rate** — Does the product's sales page do the heavy lifting, or are you expected to do all the persuading yourself? Check if the program shares any conversion data. If they don't, look for independent reviews from other affiliates.

**Purchase intent** — Are people actively searching for this product or something like it? A product that people discover passively is a much harder sell than one people are already Googling at 11pm because they need to fix a problem.

New affiliates chase commission rates. Experienced affiliates chase conversion rates and buyer intent. That gap in thinking explains most of the difference in results.

## The "Would I Buy This?" Test Is Not Enough

Here's a filter a lot of beginners use: "I'd personally buy this, so other people probably would too." It's a reasonable starting point but it's not a strategy.

Your personal taste tells you almost nothing about whether a product will convert for a specific audience in a specific context.

What you actually need to ask is: **"Would the specific person I'm writing for buy this, right now, at this price, given what they already know?"** That question forces you to think about buying stage, price sensitivity, and awareness level — all things that affect whether someone clicks your link and completes a purchase.

A practical shortcut here: spend time in the communities where your target audience hangs out. Reddit threads. Facebook groups. YouTube comment sections. Look for the questions people ask repeatedly, the frustrations they vent about, the products they mention by name. That's real market research, and it takes maybe an hour to do properly.

## Matching Product Type to Your Content Style Actually Matters

Not all affiliate products work with all content formats. This is something nobody really talks about, but it explains a lot of quiet failures.

Physical products work well with review content, comparison posts, and "best of" lists. They tend to have lower commission rates but higher trust levels — people are comfortable buying physical stuff online.

Digital products and courses convert better when you can demonstrate the transformation or outcome they promise. If you can't explain clearly what changes for someone after they use it, your audience won't buy it either.

SaaS and software tools are great for tutorial content. If you can show someone how to use the tool and solve a real problem in the process, the affiliate link feels like a natural next step rather than a pitch.

Think about the content you're most comfortable making. Then work backwards to product types that naturally fit that format. If you hate writing reviews, don't build a strategy around physical product affiliate links. If you like teaching through tutorials and walkthroughs, SaaS affiliate programs might be your sweet spot.

Forcing a mismatch between your content style and your product type creates friction at every stage. It makes the content harder to write, harder to rank, and harder to convert.

## The Shortcut That Isn't: Joining Every Program at Once

When you're new, it feels like momentum to sign up for ten affiliate programs in one afternoon. It's not. It's a really effective way to spread yourself thin and measure nothing.

Pick one product in one niche and go deep before you go wide. Create content specifically designed to attract the right buyer for that one product. Test the link placement, the call-to-action language, the context around the recommendation. Once you understand what's working — and more importantly, *why* — then you expand.

## One Filter Most Beginners Skip Completely

Before you commit to promoting anything, look at the product's refund rate and customer satisfaction signals.

This sounds obvious but most beginners skip it entirely. If a product has a high refund rate, it will hurt your credibility with your audience and in some programs it can actually claw back your commissions. Neither outcome is good.

How do you check? Search the product name plus "review", "refund", "complaint" or "scam" on Google and YouTube. Check Trustpilot if it exists. Look at what real customers are saying, not just the testimonials on the sales page.

You want to promote products that over-deliver on their promise. Because when someone buys through your link and loves what they get, they trust *you* more. They come back to your content. They buy through your links again. That compounding trust is the actual long game of affiliate marketing, and it only works if the products you recommend are genuinely good.

This filter takes fifteen minutes. Most beginners skip it. Don't be most beginners.

## Your Next Step

Here's what to do right now — not this week, right now:

**1. Pick one audience, one problem.** Write it out in one sentence: "I'm helping [specific person] solve [specific problem]." If you can't write that sentence, you're not ready to pick a product yet.

**2. Spend 45 minutes in communities where that audience hangs out.** Reddit, Facebook groups, YouTube comments. Note every product they mention. Note every question they ask repeatedly. This is your product shortlist.

**3. Apply the three filters before you sign up for anything.** Does the product have strong buyer intent behind it? Does it match your content style? Does it have decent customer satisfaction signals? If it passes all three — that's your starting product.

You don't need to get this perfect. You need to get it *intentional*. The beginners who make it through the first few months aren't necessarily the smartest or the most talented. They're the ones who made deliberate choices instead of random ones.

Start deliberate. The rest gets easier from there.
