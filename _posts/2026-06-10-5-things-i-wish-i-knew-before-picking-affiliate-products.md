# 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before Picking Affiliate Products

You joined three affiliate programs, picked products that looked popular, slapped some links in a blog post, and waited. Nothing happened. Not even a click. And now you're wondering if affiliate marketing is actually real or just another thing the internet lied to you about.

Here's what nobody told you upfront: the product selection phase is where most beginners silently fail — before they write a single word of content.

---

## The Mistake That Costs Most Beginners Three Months

Most people pick products based on commission rates. I did this. I found a program paying 40% recurring commissions and thought I'd cracked the code. Spoiler: I hadn't.

The problem is that high commissions don't mean high conversions. A product nobody is searching for, or one that solves a problem your audience doesn't actually have, will earn you exactly zero regardless of what percentage you'd get paid.

Commission rate is the last thing you should be looking at. Start with the problem the product solves. Then work backwards to figure out whether your audience has that problem, whether they're actively looking for a solution, and whether this particular product is something they'd actually trust. The commission conversation comes after all of that.

This one mindset shift would have saved me an embarrassing amount of time in my first few months.

---

## Why "Promote What You Love" Is Incomplete Advice

You've heard it a hundred times: promote products you're passionate about. And honestly? It's not wrong. But it's not the whole story either.

Passion without demand is a dead end.

Say you love a niche productivity app that costs $8/month. You genuinely think it's great. But if only 200 people a month are searching for anything related to it, you're fighting for scraps in an empty room. Passion has to meet search intent.

The combination you're looking for is: something you understand well enough to write about authentically + a real, searchable problem it solves + an audience willing to spend money. All three. Not two out of three.

When you find that overlap, content creation becomes easier because you're not performing enthusiasm — you actually have it. And readers can feel the difference.

## How to Vet a Product Before You Commit to It

This is the part nobody writes a checklist for. So let me give you one.

Before you decide to promote anything, run it through these five questions:

**1. Would you buy it yourself?** Not "would you consider it" — would you actually spend your own money on it? If the answer is no, your readers will sense that hesitation in your writing.

**2. Is there real search volume around the problem it solves?** Use Google's autocomplete, AnswerThePublic, or even Reddit to see if people are actively asking about this problem. You want existing demand, not a problem you have to convince people they have.

**3. Does the product have decent reviews outside of its own website?** Check Trustpilot, Reddit threads, YouTube comments. Real users are ruthless, and you'll learn things the sales page conveniently leaves out.

**4. What does the sales page look like?** You're sending people there. If it looks outdated, confusing, or overly aggressive, your conversion rate will suffer regardless of how good your content is.

**5. What's the cookie window?** A 24-hour cookie window means if someone clicks your link today and buys tomorrow, you get paid. A 90-day window means you have three months of credit. This matters more than most beginners realise.

If a product passes all five, you've got something worth your time. If it fails two or more, move on without guilt. There are thousands of programs out there.

## The Niche Trap That Nobody Warns You About

Here's an uncomfortable truth: going too broad kills you, but so does going too narrow.

"Personal finance" is too broad. "Budgeting apps for single moms in their 30s who just started their first job" is too narrow. You want something in the middle — specific enough that you're talking to a real person with a real problem, broad enough that there's actual traffic to compete for.

A beginner-friendly approach is to pick a category first (personal finance, software tools, health, e-commerce) and then narrow down to a specific audience within it. Who are they? What stage of life are they in? What's their budget? What do they already believe about the problem?

When you know who you're writing for that clearly, product selection gets easier. You stop asking "is this product good?" and start asking "is this product right for this specific person?" That's a much easier question to answer.

## The Numbers Game You Need To Understand Early

Let's talk about money — not in a hype way, but in a realistic way.

Most affiliate programs pay between 5% and 50% commission. Physical products (think Amazon) sit at the low end. Digital products, software, and courses tend to be higher. Recurring commission programs — where you earn every month the customer stays subscribed — are the holy grail for building passive income over time.

Here's a rough way to think about it:

- A $20 product at 30% commission = $6 per sale
- You'd need 167 sales per month to hit $1,000

That sounds like a lot. And it is, until you factor in that a well-placed article can generate sales passively for years. One article I wrote two years ago still earns a few hundred dollars a month. That's the real game — not quick wins, but compounding content.

The math also tells you something practical: if you're in the early stages and working with limited content output, focus on higher-ticket products or recurring commission programs. Getting 15 sales of a $100 product is more achievable than getting 167 sales of a $20 one.

## Trust Is The Only Currency That Actually Matters

This is the thing I wish someone had said to me on day one.

Your audience doesn't owe you anything. They clicked your link because they trusted your recommendation. The moment you promote something mediocre for a commission, that trust evaporates — and it doesn't come back easily.

I learned this the hard way. Early on, I promoted a course I hadn't finished because the commission was good. A reader bought it, hated it, and emailed me about it. That felt awful. I refunded them out of my own pocket and removed the link the same day.

Be honest about limitations. Say when something is expensive. Say when a free alternative exists. Recommend the paid option when it genuinely offers more value — not because you need the sale.

Counter-intuitive as it sounds, honesty is what builds an audience that buys from you repeatedly. One reader who trusts you is worth more than a hundred who clicked a link once and never came back.

## Your Next Step

You don't need to have everything figured out. You just need a starting point that isn't random.

Here's what to do right now:

**1. Pick one problem you understand well.** Not a product. A problem. Write it down in plain language — the way someone would type it into Google at 11pm when they're frustrated. That's your starting point.

**2. Find three products that solve that problem and run them through the five-question vetting list above.** You're not committing to anything yet. You're just doing recon. See which one you'd actually recommend to a friend.

**3. Write one piece of content — one article, one post, one video — that addresses the problem honestly.** Mention the product naturally. Don't sell. Just help.

That's the loop. It's not complicated, but it does take patience. The people who win at affiliate marketing aren't the ones who found a magic product — they're the ones who kept showing up for their audience long after the excitement wore off.

You can do that. Start with the problem.
